PROSE
----------------------------------------------------------------

The configuration that the model was trained with at the start of
the project, and which served as the fixed baseline for both the
grid search and the two follow-up experiment phases described
below, was as follows. The training objective was binary
cross-entropy on next-item prediction with a single negative item
sampled per non-padding position. Negatives were drawn uniformly
from the item catalogue, rejecting any item that was already in
the user's full interaction history (training, validation, and
test items combined) or that equalled the current positive target;
this exclusion criterion was tightened later (Paragraph 4).
Padding positions were masked out of the loss. Optimisation used
Adam at PyTorch defaults ($\beta_1 = 0.9$, $\beta_2 = 0.999$) with
a small weight decay of $10^{-4}$, a constant learning rate of
$1\!\times\!10^{-3}$, and gradient-norm clipping at $5.0$ applied
before every parameter update. A single dropout rate of $0.2$ was
shared across the model. Training ran for a maximum of $50$
epochs with mini-batch size $128$ and early stopping on validation
NDCG@10 at patience $5$. A fixed random seed of $42$ was set
across Python's \texttt{random} module, NumPy, and PyTorch
(including its CUDA backend) so identical configurations yield
bit-identical metrics across runs.

Hyperparameter selection then proceeded in three stages, each
using the baseline above as the starting point. The first stage
was a full grid search over four architectural hyperparameters
specified for ablation by the assignment: number of Transformer
blocks $\in \{1, 2, 3\}$, hidden dimension $\in \{64, 128, 256\}$,
number of attention heads $\in \{1, 2, 4\}$, and maximum sequence
length $\in \{50, 100, 200\}$, for a total of $3^4 = 81$
configurations. Configurations were ranked by validation NDCG@10
and the top three were retained for further study. The second
stage applied focused training-time tuning to those top three
configurations: dropout was swept at $\{0.1, 0.5\}$ against the
existing $0.2$ baseline, and learning rate at $\{5\!\times\!10^{-4},
10^{-2}\}$ against the existing $10^{-3}$ baseline, with the
architecture held fixed. The learning rate of $10^{-3}$ was
confirmed as best. The dropout sweep was less conclusive:
dropout $0.5$ outperformed dropout $0.1$ head-to-head, but did not
consistently beat the original dropout $0.2$ once the post-search
optimiser refinement of Paragraph 3 was also applied, so dropout
$0.2$ was retained in the final configuration. The third stage
explored a longer training budget (100 epochs, patience 10) and a
larger maximum sequence length ($200$) on the top configurations
at dropout $0.5$; neither change improved validation NDCG@10,
indicating that the chosen architecture was not constrained by
sequence length or training budget. The architecture selected at
the end of this three-stage process was two Transformer blocks,
$128$ hidden units, four attention heads, and a maximum sequence
length of $50$, achieving a validation NDCG@10 of $0.026538$ on
the unmodified baseline.

With the architecture fixed, we then tested a small number of
training-side refinements one at a time, retaining each only if
it improved validation NDCG@10. The first retained change was a
two-part adjustment to the Adam optimiser made together because
both come from the official SASRec implementation [2]: the
second-moment momentum coefficient was changed from the PyTorch
default of $\beta_2 = 0.999$ to $\beta_2 = 0.98$, and the weight
decay was removed (from $10^{-4}$ to $0$). The faster second-moment
estimate stabilises the optimiser during early epochs while item
embeddings are still close to their initial values, and the
removal of weight decay matches the official SASRec, which relies
on dropout alone as the regulariser. Together this change moved
validation NDCG@10 from $0.026538$ to $0.026883$ (a relative
improvement of $1.3\%$).

A second retained change concerned negative sampling. The original
sampler rejected items in the user's full train + validation +
test history when drawing negatives; this constituted a small
information leak from the held-out splits into the training
objective, since the negative set was being kept aware of which
items were targets of the validation and test sets. We aligned the
sampler with the official SASRec implementation [2] by rejecting
only items in the user's \emph{training} history. Closing this
leak moved validation NDCG@10 from $0.026883$ back down to
$0.026540$, almost exactly cancelling the gain from the optimiser
change above; the headline metric is therefore very close to the
grid-search baseline of $0.026538$, but is now produced under a
defensible evaluation protocol with no held-out information
visible to the training loss. We also tested increasing the
per-position negative count from $K = 1$ to $K = 100$, motivated
by an observed plateau in training loss; this did not improve
validation NDCG@10 (it dropped slightly by $0.75\%$, although test
NDCG@10 did improve by $3.2\%$) and was reverted to maintain a
single-source model-selection criterion. This attempt is discussed
in detail in Section~4.

The final training schedule retained the baseline values of $50$
epochs maximum, mini-batch size $128$, patience $5$, and seed
$42$. We verified that $50$ epochs is sufficient by re-running the
selected configuration with $100$ epochs and patience $10$; the
resulting metrics matched the $50$-epoch run to six decimal
places, confirming that the model converges well before the
budget is exhausted. The checkpoint with the highest validation
NDCG@10 observed during training is retained and used for final
test evaluation. The full final configuration used for all
results in Section~3 is therefore: two Transformer blocks; $128$
hidden units; four attention heads; maximum sequence length $50$;
dropout $0.2$; learning rate $10^{-3}$; Adam with $\beta_2 = 0.98$
and no weight decay; gradient clipping at $5.0$; one negative
item per position sampled with training-history exclusion; a
maximum of $50$ epochs with early stopping at patience $5$;
mini-batch size $128$; and random seed $42$.
