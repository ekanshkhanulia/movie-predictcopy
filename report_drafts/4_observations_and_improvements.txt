OPENING PARAGRAPH
----------------------------------------------------------------

This section discusses what we observed beyond the headline numbers.
We focus on three threads. First, we trace why the final architecture
selected by the grid search is at the size it is, rather than larger
or smaller, and why extending the training budget beyond $50$ epochs
did not help. Second, we report three design choices we tested in
isolation and either discarded or kept under careful caveats; these
results clarify which Transformer-era tricks port cleanly to our
PyTorch implementation of SASRec and which do not. Third, we describe
a diagnosed training-loss plateau and the unsuccessful attempt to
break it by increasing the negative-sample count, which led to the
hard-negative-mining direction proposed in Section~4.4. Each
subsection ends with a one-sentence takeaway in italics.

----------------------------------------------------------------
4.1 Architecture is not the bottleneck
----------------------------------------------------------------

The architectural grid search was conducted over $3^4 = 81$
configurations (\#blocks $\in\{1,2,3\}$, hidden $\in\{64,128,256\}$,
heads $\in\{1,2,4\}$, maxlen $\in\{50,100,200\}$). The top three
configurations differ only in number of attention heads and number
of blocks; all three use 128 hidden units and a maximum sequence
length of 50. Larger hidden dimensions (256) did not improve
validation NDCG@10, suggesting that the model is not capacity-limited
on MovieLens 1M's ${\sim}3{,}706$-item catalogue. Longer sequences
(maxlen 100, 200) also failed to improve over maxlen 50, which is
consistent with the heavy-tailed distribution of user history lengths
in MovieLens 1M: the median user has fewer than 50 positive
interactions after the rating $\geq 4$ filter, so most of the
additional positions in a length-200 sequence are pad tokens that
must be masked anyway.

We separately verified that the 50-epoch training budget is not the
bottleneck either. Re-running the best configuration with 100 epochs
and patience 10 produced metrics identical to the 50-epoch run to
six decimal places, because the saved checkpoint is the one with the
highest validation NDCG@10 ever observed and that peak is reached
well before epoch 50.

\textit{Takeaway: neither model size, sequence length, nor training
length is the limiting factor; the bottleneck lies in the training
signal itself.}

----------------------------------------------------------------
4.2 Negative results from training-side tweaks
----------------------------------------------------------------

We tested two training- and architecture-side tricks that are
standard practice elsewhere in the Transformer literature and found
them counterproductive in our setting. We document them here both to
contextualise the design space we explored and to support the
takeaway above.

\textbf{Linear learning-rate warmup.} A 1000-step linear warmup of
the learning rate, in the spirit of the original Transformer, was
added on top of the post-grid-search baseline. Validation NDCG@10
dropped by $6.8\%$ relative (from $0.026883$ to $0.025061$). The
problem is one of scale: a 50-epoch run on our dataset contains
roughly $50 \times 47 = 2{,}350$ optimiser steps in total, of which
$1{,}000$ would be at a reduced learning rate -- about $42\%$ of the
training budget. With patience $5$, the model was early-stopped
before it could recover from the slow start. Warmup is meant to be
$5{-}10\%$ of total training; copying the schedule from a paper that
trains for $200{+}$ epochs without rescaling produces a warmup window
much longer than the available budget. We reverted this change.

\textbf{Item-embedding scaling by $\sqrt{d}$.} The original
Transformer multiplies the input embedding by $\sqrt{d_{\text{model}}}$
before adding positional embeddings, and the official SASRec
implementation [2] adopts the same convention. Adding this scaling to
our PyTorch port reduced validation NDCG@10 by $0.9\%$ (from
$0.026540$ to $0.026300$) and degraded $7$ of $8$ headline metrics.
The cause is an initialisation mismatch: TensorFlow's default
embedding initialiser produces values with a small standard deviation
(typically near $0.01$), so multiplying by $\sqrt{128} \approx 11.3$
brings the item-embedding magnitude up to roughly the positional
embedding's scale. PyTorch's default \texttt{nn.Embedding}
initialisation uses $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$, which is already at unit
scale, and multiplying by $11.3$ makes the item signal roughly an
order of magnitude larger than the positional signal -- the opposite
imbalance from what the trick is designed to correct. We reverted
this change.

\textit{Takeaway: tricks lifted from the official TensorFlow SASRec
codebase or from the original Transformer paper sometimes interact
badly with PyTorch defaults. They are worth testing, but should not
be assumed to transfer.}

----------------------------------------------------------------
4.3 A diagnosed plateau, and an unsuccessful K=100 attempt
----------------------------------------------------------------

The most informative single experiment in our project was an
attempt to break the validation plateau by sampling more negatives
per position. We describe the diagnosis, the change, and the
result honestly here, because the result was mixed and the lesson
is in the mixture.

With $K = 1$ negative per position, the per-epoch validation
NDCG@10 trajectory peaks around epoch $17$ at approximately
$0.0265$ and remains flat thereafter. Training loss saturates at
approximately $0.88$ during the same window and does not decrease
further. For a binary cross-entropy loss with sigmoid scores,
$\log 2 \approx 0.693$ is the value of the loss when both the
positive logit and the negative logit sit at zero -- i.e.\ when
the model is making no decision at all. A plateau slightly above
$\log 2$ indicates that, although the model is learning to push
positives up modestly, it is not making useful progress on pushing
negatives down.

The hypothesised cause is that, with only ${\sim}3{,}706$
candidate items and ${\sim}50{-}200$ items per user history, a
uniformly sampled negative is overwhelmingly likely to be a
"trivial" negative -- an item the user has zero interest in, that
the model already scores low. With $K = 1$ each gradient step
sees only one negative per position, and the optimiser barely
has to push that negative below the positive. Increasing $K$
exposes the model to many more negatives per step. We therefore
tested $K = 100$, with the BCE loss averaged over the $K$
dimension to keep the per-position negative scale balanced with
the positive term.

The empirical result was mixed rather than clean. Validation
NDCG@10 \emph{drifted down} by $0.75\%$, from $0.026540$ to
$0.026341$, and validation Recall@10 fell by $1.6\%$. Test
metrics moved in the opposite direction at the top of the
ranking: test NDCG@10 \emph{improved} by $3.2\%$ (from $0.020831$
to $0.021494$) and test Recall@10 by $2.4\%$, while test top-20
metrics moved down slightly. Because we are obliged to select the
final model on validation NDCG@10 alone, $K = 100$ does not meet
the selection criterion and was reverted; the final model uses
$K = 1$. We document the result here rather than in Section~3
because it is informative beyond the headline number.

The interpretation is that simply sampling \emph{more} negatives
of the same kind is insufficient to break the plateau on a
catalogue this small. With only ${\sim}3{,}706$ items, even at
$K = 100$ approximately ninety-five of every hundred draws are
trivial negatives that contribute almost no gradient. The
test-side improvement is suggestive of a small generalisation
benefit (the contrastive signal is no longer dominated by the
handful of negatives that happen to rank near val items at
$K = 1$), but the validation drop is the metric we are obliged
to optimise.

\textit{Takeaway: increasing $K$ on uniform negatives is
necessary but not sufficient. The negatives themselves need to
be \emph{harder} -- popularity-based, neighbour-based, or
model-aware -- before $K$ becomes a useful lever. This motivates
the hard-negative-mining suggestion in Section~4.4.}

----------------------------------------------------------------
4.4 Suggestions for further improvement
----------------------------------------------------------------

Time and compute prevented us from testing the following directions,
each of which is independently motivated by our observations and
would be a natural next step.

\textbf{Hard negative mining.} Sampling negatives from popular items
or from items topically similar to the user's history (e.g.\ via
co-occurrence) is well known to provide stronger gradients than
uniform sampling and is a natural extension of the K = 100 result
above. Whatever fraction of the $100$ negatives end up being
informative under uniform sampling, hard mining would push that
fraction toward $100\%$ at the cost of additional bookkeeping.

\textbf{BPR (pairwise ranking) loss.} BCE optimises a pointwise
calibration of scores, but our evaluation metrics (Recall@K, NDCG@K)
are pure ranking metrics. A pairwise BPR loss directly optimises the
score margin between positive and negative and is a closer match to
the evaluation objective.

\textbf{Properly masked attention over padding.} Our current
implementation enables the causal attention mask but disables the
\texttt{key\_padding\_mask}, because enabling it produces NaN logits
on early positions of left-padded sequences (the row of the
attention matrix at position $0$ has all keys masked, so
$\mathrm{softmax}(-\infty)$ is undefined). A correct implementation
would either use a "safe softmax" (substituting a finite default
distribution when the mask covers all keys) or right-pad sequences
at the data-loading layer. Either fix would prevent any attention
weight from being placed on padding positions.

\textbf{Cosine learning-rate decay.} A milder schedule than the
linear warmup we tested (which failed for budget reasons described
in Section~4.2) would be cosine decay over the full 50-epoch budget.
This is sized correctly for our training run by construction and is
known to provide modest improvements in similar settings without
risking the early-stop interaction that hurt warmup.
