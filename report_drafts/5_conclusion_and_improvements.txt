----------------------------------------------------------------
PROSE
----------------------------------------------------------------

We presented a PyTorch implementation of SASRec [2] for the
MovieLens 1M next-item recommendation task, and described the
chronological process by which we arrived at the final
configuration: an architectural grid search over $3^4 = 81$
combinations, followed by training-time tuning of dropout and
learning rate on the top three architectures, followed by a
series of single-change ablations on the best architecture
covering optimiser settings, negative sampling, and several
training-time tricks. The selected model -- two Transformer
blocks, $128$ hidden units, four attention heads, maximum
sequence length $50$, dropout $0.2$, learning rate $10^{-3}$,
Adam with $\beta_2 = 0.98$ and no weight decay, gradient
clipping at $5.0$, and one negative item per position drawn with
training-history exclusion -- achieves a test NDCG@10 of
$0.0208$ and a test Recall@10 of $0.0418$ under the
full-ranking evaluation protocol mandated by the assignment.

The most useful methodological observations from the project
were the negative ones. Two tricks that are standard in related
literature, linear learning-rate warmup and $\sqrt{d}$ embedding
scaling, turned out to interact badly with our PyTorch defaults
and short training budget; both were discarded after one-change
ablations and are documented as negative results rather than
included in the final model. A diagnosed plateau in the BCE
training loss motivated an attempt to sample $K = 100$ negatives
per position rather than $K = 1$, which also failed to improve
validation NDCG@10 on this small catalogue, suggesting that the
limiting factor is the \emph{difficulty} of the negatives rather
than their count. The most promising directions for further
improvement, listed in Section~4.4, are hard negative mining
(popularity- or model-based), a BPR pairwise ranking loss, and
correctly-masked attention over padding tokens that does not
fall back to the current zero-out workaround.

We note that the absolute NDCG values reported above are not
directly comparable to those quoted in the original SASRec paper
[2]. Two assignment-mandated choices make the task strictly
harder in our setting: the rating $\geq 4$ binarisation removes
implicit-feedback signal that the original work uses, and the
full-ranking evaluation protocol scores the held-out item
against all $\sim\!3{,}706$ catalogue items rather than against
$100$ sampled negatives. Section~4 discusses this comparison in
more detail.

----------------------------------------------------------------
ONE-PARAGRAPH ALTERNATIVE (if you need to cut harder for space)
----------------------------------------------------------------

We presented a PyTorch implementation of SASRec [2] for
next-item recommendation on MovieLens 1M, selected through an
$81$-cell architectural grid search and a series of single-change
training-side ablations. The final model achieves a test
NDCG@10 of $0.0208$ and a test Recall@10 of $0.0418$ under the
full-ranking evaluation protocol. Our main methodological
finding is that, on a small-catalogue sequential dataset,
training-time decisions (the negative-sampling protocol and a
pair of Adam hyperparameters) influence final performance more
than architectural choices, and that several literature-standard
training tricks (LR warmup, $\sqrt{d}$ scaling, increased
negative count) interact badly with our short training budget
and small item catalogue; supporting evidence is in Section~4.
Hard negative mining, a pairwise ranking loss, and a properly-
masked attention over padding tokens are the most promising
directions for further work.
